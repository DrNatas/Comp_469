== Learning Targets

Students should be able to:

* Explain why full joint distributions become difficult to specify directly as the number of variables grows.
* Define Bayesian networks as directed acyclic graphs with local probability information.
* Interpret nodes, directed links, parents, conditional probability tables, and prior probabilities.
* Explain how Bayesian network topology represents conditional independence assumptions.
* Use the burglary-alarm example to identify direct causes, evidence variables, and hidden variables.
* Explain the product form semantics of Bayesian networks.
* Describe the chain rule and the construction method for Bayesian networks.
* Explain why causal node orderings often produce more compact and natural networks than diagnostic orderings.
* Identify non-descendants, Markov blankets, and d-separation relationships.
* Explain compact conditional distribution patterns such as deterministic nodes, context-specific independence, and noisy-OR.
* Describe how Bayesian networks can represent continuous and hybrid domains.
* Explain linear-Gaussian, probit, and expit/logit models at a conceptual level.
* Analyze the car insurance case study in terms of hidden variables, evidence variables, and causal structure.
* Compare exact inference by enumeration with variable elimination.
* Explain factors, pointwise products, summing out variables, and the role of dynamic programming in inference.
